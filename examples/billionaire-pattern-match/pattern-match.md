# Pattern Match — Which Playbook Fits Which User

> Midas compares the four users' signal patterns against the three famous-model wealth operating systems and scores the fit.

---

## The Matching Framework

Midas uses the six wealth OS dimensions to score each user against each model:

1. **Money Engine** — Where does revenue come from? Linear or exponential? One-time or compounding?
2. **Deal Architecture** — Own money or OPM? Who bears risk vs. who captures upside?
3. **Investment Thesis** — Value, growth, or arbitrage? What metric matters most?
4. **Risk Profile** — Concentrated or diversified? Has the person survived near-zero?
5. **Leverage Model** — Capital, brand, tech, network, or political leverage?
6. **Exit Pattern** — IPO, M&A, dividends, never-exit? Where does capital go next?

Each dimension gets a 0–5 score for how well the user's emerging situation matches each model. The highest total wins the match.

## User A — Marcus (Slack Mining)

### Profile

Skilled engineer inside mid-size company, accumulating unpaid internal-tools expertise over a year, now being approached by multiple stakeholders to solve specific problems. Has working prototypes of better tools (shadow spreadsheet, AWS audit approach). Lives in a stable W-2 situation with low personal risk tolerance (no explicit signals of willingness to bet existentially).

| Dimension | Musk | Buffett | Thiel |
|---|---|---|---|
| Money Engine | 1 — Marcus is not producing exponential vision premium; he's producing linear billable hours for specific problems. | 4 — Cash-flowing, boring, predictable inside a circle of competence. | 3 — There's a small monopoly possibility in "the person who fixes broken internal tooling," but it's narrow. |
| Deal Architecture | 1 — No OPM, no vision-based financing. | 5 — Own capital (time), low debt, direct delivery. | 2 — No venture structure in sight. |
| Investment Thesis | 1 — No first-principles rebuild thesis. | 5 — Value inside a known domain. | 3 — Arguably a micro-monopoly in "internal tooling consulting for mid-size tech companies." |
| Risk Profile | 0 — Not existentially concentrated. Still W-2. | 4 — Conservative concentration inside known capability. | 2 — Not aggressive enough on the asymmetric bet. |
| Leverage Model | 1 — No brand leverage, no political leverage, no technology platform. | 4 — Reputation leverage within a trusted circle. | 2 — Small network leverage, not Thiel-scale. |
| Exit Pattern | 0 — No exit plan because this is service work. | 5 — Hold forever, accumulate clients, let compounding do the work. | 1 — No product / no equity. |
| **TOTAL** | **4/30** | **27/30** | **13/30** |

### Match result

**Buffett — overwhelming fit.**

Marcus's situation maps almost perfectly onto the Buffett playbook: specific expertise inside a specific circle of competence, boring and predictable cash flows, conservative concentration, long holding periods with existing customers, and reputation-based leverage. He is not going to become the next Musk (no vision premium to sell) or Thiel (no monopoly category to define). But he can absolutely become a specialized consulting practice that compounds reputation and clients over decades.

## Notable Observation

**Musk doesn't match any of the four users.**

This is important for Midas to call out explicitly. The Musk playbook requires:
- Existential risk tolerance
- Ability to raise vision-premium capital
- Operating at an industry-rebuild scale
- Technology platform leverage

None of the four users in this example have those structural conditions. Every one of them would fail if they tried to apply the Musk playbook to their situation.

This is a critical Midas lesson: **playbook matching is about structural fit, not aspiration.** The Musk playbook is only the right playbook for people operating under Musk-like structural conditions. For everyone else, trying to apply it is a trap — they'll take on existential risk without the vision-capital access that makes the risk survivable.

Most Midas users will match Buffett or Thiel, not Musk. That's not a limitation of the playbooks — it's a reality of the structural conditions most people actually live inside.
