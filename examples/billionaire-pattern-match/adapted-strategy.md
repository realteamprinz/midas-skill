# Adapted Strategy — Playbooks Translated to Each User's Situation

> Midas's final output: for each user, a concrete adaptation of the best-matching famous-model playbook, translated into a 6-dimension strategy using the user's actual circumstances.

The point of the famous-model pattern matching isn't to tell users to imitate billionaires. It's to **steal the structurally relevant pieces of proven playbooks** and apply them to the user's actual situation. This is the adaptation layer.

---

## User A — Marcus (Slack Mining): Buffett-Adapted Playbook

### The core translation

Marcus's Buffett fit (27/30) is overwhelming. The adaptation is straightforward: **run a Buffett-style specialist consulting practice inside a circle of competence that's already been validated by his current employer's problems.**

Here's the six-dimension playbook adapted to Marcus's specific reality:

### 1. Money Engine (adapted)

**Buffett original:** Insurance float → value acquisitions → compounding returns forever.

**Marcus adaptation:** His "float" is his day-job salary — a permanent, low-risk pool of capital he doesn't need to touch for living expenses. This lets him take on consulting engagements purely for the upside, without ever being forced to accept a bad deal for survival reasons. The money engine becomes:

```
W-2 Salary (covers living)
  ↓
Moonlight Engagements (pure upside capital)
  ↓
Reinvest into capability (better tools, certifications, portfolio artifacts)
  ↓
More engagements at higher rates
  ↓
Eventually, enough recurring clients to transition out of W-2
```

The W-2 job IS Marcus's float. Do not quit. Use it as the structural advantage.

### 2. Deal Architecture (adapted)

**Buffett original:** No debt at holding level, fair price for great assets, permanent commitment.

**Marcus adaptation:** No debt, no external capital, no investors. Every engagement is a direct contract between Marcus and the client, paid in cash or bank transfer, invoiced on delivery. No venture architecture needed.

**Key decision rule:** Fixed-fee engagements only, never hourly. Buffett's aversion to variable costs applies here — fixed-fee contracts force Marcus to price based on value delivered, not hours worked, which protects him from scope creep and rewards his efficiency.

### 3. Investment Thesis (adapted)

**Buffett original:** Value inside circle of competence, long holding periods, moat-protected businesses.

**Marcus adaptation:** Marcus's "investment" is his time. Each engagement is a micro-investment of hours into a client relationship. The thesis is: **invest in clients who will refer more clients** (lifetime value > single engagement value), and **invest in engagements that generate reusable artifacts** (every AWS audit produces a template that makes the next one faster).

### 4. Risk Profile (adapted)

**Buffett original:** Rule #1 never lose money. Concentrated in things that survive near-zero.

**Marcus adaptation:** Do not take on engagements that risk the W-2 job. Do not sign NDAs that conflict with employment agreements. Do not use company IP in side work. Do not take on a client so important that losing them would be catastrophic. Every engagement should be a small enough slice of Marcus's total picture that if it fails, he shrugs and moves on.

### 5. Leverage Model (adapted)

**Buffett original:** Cheap permanent capital (float) + reputation leverage.

**Marcus adaptation:** Marcus's leverage is his **insider knowledge of how mid-size tech companies actually break**. Every year at his day job, he accumulates more pattern-recognition for which tools are broken, which processes leak money, and which fixes work. That institutional knowledge is a moat competitors can't acquire without also working inside such companies.

### 6. Exit Pattern (adapted)

**Buffett original:** Never exit. Hold forever.

**Marcus adaptation:** Marcus should be running the practice 20 years from now, not flipping it to someone else in 3 years. No growth for growth's sake. No scaling to a 10-person agency. Stay small, stay specialist, let compounding do the work, and treat every client relationship as if it will last a decade.

### Concrete first 90 days

- **Week 1:** DM Sarah with a fixed-fee AWS audit proposal (Opportunity #1 from Slack mining)
- **Week 2:** Deliver the AWS audit, document the process
- **Week 4:** Offer the same service to one friend at another mid-size tech company, at the same fixed price
- **Week 6:** Publish a 1-page case study (anonymized) on LinkedIn
- **Week 12:** Have 3 paid engagements closed, plus a small portfolio of written artifacts

**No quitting, no LLC yet, no website, no branding.** Just three paid engagements and one case study. The rest comes in Months 4–12.

## User B — Woodworker (Photo Mining): Buffett-Adapted Playbook

### The core translation

Woodworker's Buffett fit is also 27/30. The adaptation is similar in structure but different in execution because the product is physical.

### 1. Money Engine (adapted)

**Buffett original:** Float + value acquisitions + compounding.

**Woodworker adaptation:** The "float" is the existing day-job income (if any) plus the 50% deposits on each commission (free working capital provided by customers). Every new commission starts with half the money already in the bank, which covers materials and removes delivery risk.

```
Commission deposits (upfront)
  ↓
Materials + tools (COGS)
  ↓
Delivered piece
  ↓
Balance payment on delivery
  ↓
Reputation + referrals → more commissions
```

### 2. Deal Architecture (adapted)

- 50% deposit on order confirmation (non-refundable beyond 7-day cooling-off period)
- Balance due on delivery, before installation
- Written contract for every commission above $500
- No discounts, no deadline compression for "rush" jobs unless priced 1.5x

### 3. Investment Thesis (adapted)

The investment is in **reputation over volume**. Every piece should be photographed professionally, tagged with the customer's permission (for testimonials), and used as a distribution artifact for the next commission. The long-term customer value is measured in referrals, not in repeat purchases — most customers will only commission one piece in their life, but each satisfied customer can drive 3–5 referrals.

### 4. Risk Profile (adapted)

- Don't quit the day job. Running a side practice keeps household cash flow stable.
- Don't take on more than 2 concurrent commissions in the first year.
- Don't accept commissions outside the user's demonstrated capability (no chairs until the user has completed 10+ successful small pieces).
- Keep material costs tracked separately from personal finance, even before formal LLC registration.

### 5. Leverage Model (adapted)

- **Geographic leverage:** the user is the only trusted custom woodworker in their immediate neighborhood. Don't scale beyond the zip code for the first year.
- **Morrison Woodworks partnership:** use the subcontract relationship as a quality-signal (Morrison vetting = credibility transfer).
- **Referral leverage:** every customer should be asked for a referral or testimonial on delivery day, when satisfaction is highest.

### 6. Exit Pattern (adapted)

Never exit. This is a lifetime practice. Pass down the workshop, tools, and client list to a child or apprentice when the user eventually retires. Buffett-style permanence — own the craft, own the reputation, own the local monopoly, pass it on.

### Concrete first 90 days

- **Week 1:** Reply to the kitchen island email with the template from Photos-example Opportunity #1
- **Week 1:** Send Morrison the capability sheet (Opportunity #2)
- **Week 2:** Accept the kitchen island deposit, start the build
- **Week 4:** Register a DBA and open a separate checking account
- **Week 8:** Deliver kitchen island, photograph professionally, request testimonial + referral
- **Week 12:** Have 2+ commissions delivered, 1 in progress, 1 subcontract gig from Morrison

## User C — Complaint Chat Member: Thiel-Adapted Playbook

### The core translation

User C's Thiel fit (27/30) is the highest non-Buffett match in the example set. The adaptation: **run a Thiel-style monopoly play in one neighborhood first, refuse to scale until the first neighborhood is bulletproof.**

### 1. Money Engine (adapted)

**Thiel original:** Pre-monopoly venture investments; wait for compounding; exit into next thesis.

**User C adaptation:** The money engine is subscription fees from a curated local roster. Critically, this is NOT a marketplace — it's a trusted curator who charges members for access to a pre-vetted provider list. The unit economics:

```
50 members × $15/mo = $750/mo (target at 6 months)
→ cash flow to reinvest in curation quality
→ word-of-mouth to 2nd neighborhood
→ 100 members × $15/mo = $1,500/mo
→ etc.
```

The trick is that each new neighborhood is effectively a new product launch, not an extension. Don't try to make one app for all neighborhoods — make one community for each neighborhood, sequentially.

### 2. Deal Architecture (adapted)

- Start with a Google Doc. Not an app. Not a startup. A Google Doc.
- Membership fee via Venmo, PayPal, or Stripe Payment Links — no software infrastructure needed
- Monthly auto-billing via Stripe once 20+ members
- No investors. Bootstrap until proven.

**Thiel's elegance principle:** the cheapest possible MVP. A Google Doc + a Venmo account is a fully functional neighborhood trusted-provider service. Don't add complexity until the complexity is demanded by paying customers.

### 3. Investment Thesis (adapted)

**Category creation.** There is no "neighborhood trusted-provider roster" category in the consumer's mind. The user's job is to name it, claim it, and own it. The thesis is: **trust is the product, curation is the moat, and neighborhood-level density is the defense against any scaled competitor.**

### 4. Risk Profile (adapted)

- Thiel-style asymmetric bet: test with one neighborhood. If it works, there's a template. If it doesn't, the loss is one afternoon of Google Doc work.
- Do not raise capital until there's a paid first cohort of 20+ members in one neighborhood.
- Do not expand to a second neighborhood until the first is stable (6+ months of retention data).

### 5. Leverage Model (adapted)

- **Network leverage:** the friend chat is the starting distribution channel. Every member is also a distribution node to their adjacent friend groups.
- **Curation leverage:** the value is in *who gets in*, not *who's on the list*. Every new provider should be vetted personally before going on the list.
- **Exclusivity leverage:** cap the neighborhood membership at ~100. Scarcity is a feature — if the list gets too big, trust dilutes.

### 6. Exit Pattern (adapted)

- No classic exit — this is a lifestyle business that compounds over decades in a specific geography.
- Could eventually be sold to a regional community platform if multiple neighborhoods are stable.
- Or could be held forever as a local income stream.

### Concrete first 90 days

- **Day 1:** Create the Google Doc. Populate with 6–10 trusted providers. Share to the friend chat.
- **Week 1:** Collect additions from chat members. Aim for 25+ providers across 6 categories.
- **Week 2:** Propose a $5/month membership fee to chat members. Collect first payments via Venmo.
- **Week 4:** Reach 10 paying members from the original chat.
- **Week 8:** Each of the 10 invites one friend-of-friend to join. Target 20 paying members.
- **Week 12:** First neighborhood is at 20+ paying members. Document the playbook. Start eyeing neighborhood #2.

## User D — Postgres Engineer: Thiel + Buffett Hybrid

### The core translation

User D has the only hybrid match in the example set (Thiel 24, Buffett 23). The adaptation: **run a Buffett-style specialist consulting practice with Thiel-style category positioning.**

### 1. Money Engine (Buffett-dominant)

Standard specialist consulting engine: fixed-fee engagements, compounding reputation, high referral rates in a deep niche.

### 2. Deal Architecture (Buffett-dominant)

- Fixed-fee engagements, never hourly
- 50% deposit on project start, balance on delivery
- Written scope, written deliverables, written acceptance criteria
- 30-day warranty period on production fixes

### 3. Investment Thesis (Thiel-dominant)

**This is where Thiel wins.** User D should explicitly position as **"the Postgres migration specialist,"** not as "a senior engineer who does consulting." The narrower the category, the stronger the monopoly positioning.

The Thiel playbook says: create a category nobody has named. User D creates the category "Postgres migration consultant" by:
- Publishing the "postgres-migration-checklist" GitHub repo (category claim)
- Writing a technical newsletter specifically about Postgres migrations (content moat)
- Building a named productized engagement ("The 5-Day Postgres Migration Audit") with a fixed SKU

### 4. Risk Profile (Buffett-dominant)

- Don't quit the day job until monthly consulting revenue matches salary for 3 consecutive months
- Don't take on engagements outside the specific niche (don't become a generalist)
- Moonlight discipline: contracts, separate bank accounts, tax compliance from day one

### 5. Leverage Model (Thiel-dominant)

- **Narrative leverage:** publish content consistently. The user's name should be associated with "Postgres migrations" via Google and LinkedIn searches within 12 months.
- **Network leverage:** the 6 adjacent "Independent Database Consultant" profiles on LinkedIn are all potential advisors. DM at least 3 in the first month.
- **Artifact leverage:** the GitHub checklist repo becomes the portfolio piece that closes deals.

### 6. Exit Pattern (Buffett-dominant)

Hold forever. Run the practice for 10+ years. Eventually, productize into training/courses/books as Thiel-style secondary revenue streams that compound off the reputation.

### Concrete first 90 days

- **Week 1:** Commit 5 real files to the postgres-migration-checklist repo
- **Week 1:** Propose a moonlight engagement to current employer

## The Meta-Lesson

**Notice what's common across all four adaptations:**

1. **Nobody quits their day job in the first 90 days.** Even the woodworker keeps their W-2 during the ramp.
2. **Every adaptation starts with the cheapest possible MVP.** A Google Doc. A GitHub repo. A Substack. No apps. No websites. No hiring.
3. **Every adaptation prioritizes reputation over revenue.** The first case study is worth more than the first dollar.
4. **Every adaptation is narrow.** Nobody tries to be a generalist. Nobody tries to scale before owning a defined niche.
5. **None of them are Musk plays.** The Musk playbook requires structural conditions (existential risk tolerance, access to vision-premium capital, industry-rebuild ambition) that none of these users have. Attempting to apply it would be a trap.

This is the central wisdom of pattern matching: **the right playbook is the one that matches your structural conditions, not the one you wish matched.** Buffett and Thiel worked for these users because the users were already operating inside Buffett- or Thiel-like structural conditions. Musk didn't work because the conditions don't match.

Most Midas users will match Buffett or Thiel. A rare few will match Musk. That's a feature of reality, not a limitation of Midas.

## How to Use Pattern Matching in Your Own Midas Practice

1. **Run multiple input types** (Slack + photos + chats + browsing) before attempting pattern matching. Single-input pattern matches are unreliable.
2. **Score honestly** against all three models. Don't force-fit a match to the model you aspire to.
3. **Accept the match even if it's boring.** "Your pattern is Buffett" is not a downgrade from "Your pattern is Musk" — it's a more survivable path.
4. **Translate the playbook to your actual circumstances.** The famous models are templates, not scripts. Every dimension needs adaptation.
5. **Re-run the match quarterly.** Your situation changes. Your pattern may shift. Midas should re-pattern-match after every 3 new input types are fed into the evolution log.

## The Honest Boundary (Again)

The famous-model files describe wealth patterns distilled from publicly available information. They do not represent the personal views or plans of the figures named. The pattern matching is a strategic framing tool, not financial advice.

**Before implementing any adapted strategy that involves material capital, contracts with clients, formation of legal entities, or transition away from employment, consult a licensed attorney, accountant, and (where appropriate) financial advisor in your jurisdiction.**

Midas identifies patterns. You and your licensed advisors decide whether and how to act on them.
